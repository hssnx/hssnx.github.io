Coding isn't Just About Programming Languages

Published: October 17 2024 Section: Essays Time: 7 min read
Tags: coding, programming, AI, problem solving, logic, education

When most people start learning to program, they often dive straight into the tools—learning programming languages, studying syntax, and understanding the nuances of coding frameworks. This may seem like a good starting point, but it often leads to a common issue that I've personally faced: they know the tools, they can write loops, functions, and use frameworks, but when faced with a real-world problem or trying to realize an idea, they freeze. They can’t translate their knowledge of syntax into something useful and don’t know where to begin, and this is because they've focused on learning the tool rather than truly understanding the problem. To make this clearer, let me explain the difference between knowledge and tools.

Knowledge is the foundation—it’s the understanding of a problem and the steps needed to solve it. Tools, like programming languages, are simply a way to express that solution. To illustrate, imagine you want to bake a cake. The knowledge part is knowing the recipe: mixing specific ingredients in a certain order, baking at the right temperature, and following the sequence of steps to get the desired result. This process—the steps and logic behind them—is your knowledge. The tools, such as an oven and a mixer, are secondary. They help bring the cake to life, but without understanding the recipe, having all the tools won't help. It's like having an oven and a mixer but just staring at them because you lack the understanding of how to bake the cake, even if you have all the ingredients.

This same logic applies to programming. Let’s say you want to create a port scanner. The logical steps to do this involve identifying the ports you want to scan, sending network requests to each port, analyzing the responses, and determining if a port is open or closed. This logical breakdown is your knowledge—the core understanding of what needs to be done. Once you know this, you can then use a tool like Python to actually implement these steps. Python is your oven or your mixer in this analogy; it helps execute the solution, but without understanding the logical steps, simply knowing Python won’t help you create a functional port scanner.

To drive this point home, let’s consider another example: imagine someone asks you to explain French history in the French language. To explain history effectively, you first need to understand the historical events, the dates, and all the context—the knowledge. The French language is just a tool that allows you to communicate this information. If you only know French but have no knowledge of history, you wouldn’t be able to explain anything. But if you deeply understand the history, the timelines, dates, and contexts, you could still convey the essence of it, even with mediocre French, or even using another language entirely. The content (knowledge) is what matters most, while the language is simply the medium through which you deliver it. Programming is no different. The logical understanding of what you’re trying to achieve is the key, while the programming language is just a tool for making it happen.

So, generally, to create something, realize an idea, or automate a task, start by understanding the problem. Then, create a solution in plain English, like: if X happens, then Y should follow, and after that, it's Z, and so on. This logical understanding leads you to a solution, and turning this English-written algorithm into code becomes the easy part. Think about it—there are thousands of people who know Python, but not everyone can create an AI model like ChatGPT. Creating AI models requires foundational knowledge of math, computer architecture, and so on. Computer scientists find these solutions first, then implement them using tools like Python or any other programming language.

Historically, the focus on learning languages and their syntax made sense because it was necessary. There was no other choice but to understand every line of code thoroughly and write everything yourself. Programmers worked in low-level languages, often spending hours writing something as basic as a print function. It was an enormous effort just to create something functional. At that time, creating something like a port scanner would have been a major achievement. Today, however, technology has advanced. Much of the tedious work of managing syntax and low-level operations has been abstracted away by higher-level languages, libraries, and now AI. The focus should shift from knowing every little detail of a programming language to understanding the bigger picture—how and why something works.

This is where AI comes in. AI won’t replace programmers; instead, it will empower them. AI can handle the repetitive, tedious parts of coding, such as syntax, debugging, and boilerplate code. This means that instead of spending time worrying about where a comma should go or debugging minor syntax issues, programmers can focus on understanding the problem they’re solving and designing the solution. It’s much like the evolution of mathematics. Decades ago, mathematicians would spend hours manually calculating large numbers, and these calculations themselves were considered an achievement. With calculators, we outsourced those calculations and moved on to more complex problems—finding the mass of distant stars, for example. Similarly, AI allows programmers to focus on the logic and architecture of a solution rather than the minutiae of writing code.

The future of programming isn’t about mastering syntax; it’s about mastering problem-solving and designing solutions. It’s about understanding what needs to be done and why, and using AI and other tools to help execute those solutions more efficiently. If you understand the logic behind what you’re doing, the language becomes secondary. It’s simply the medium through which you bring your ideas to life. AI will provide the tools, but the vision, the understanding, and the design of the solution—that’s still up to you. In this new era, understanding logic is the most important skill a programmer—or anyone who wants to build something—can have. It’s what allows you to truly solve problems, not just write code.

In conclusion: Understanding the logic—the "why" and "how" behind a problem—is far more important than learning a programming language itself. Learning a language comes as secondary, as you can now use AI to write code for your already designed algorithm by you.
