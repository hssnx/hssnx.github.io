The U.S. Invasion of Afghanistan

Published: November 20, 2024 Section: Essays Time: 8 min read
Tags: political commentary, Afghanistan, US invasion, reconstruction, democracy, history

What we often refer to as the 20-year "invasion of Afghanistan" is not how the U.S. defines it. For the U.S., the actual invasion lasted only 2–3 months, from October to late November 2001, under the name "Operation Enduring Freedom." The remaining 20 years, which we view as part of the invasion, were classified by the U.S. as the "reconstruction of Afghanistan.”

Let’s be honest: neither the U.S. nor any other global power would do such actions purely out of altruism to bring democracy to a landlocked country faar in South Asia. Nobody is that selflessly generous. If the U.S., out of sheer kindness, truly wanted to build Afghanistan and offer democracy to its people, they wouldn’t have invaded in the first place. However, they eventually found themselves in a unique and complicated situation.

After the 9/11 attacks, the U.S. embarked on a quest to capture Osama bin Laden, a mission even depicted in the movie Zero Dark Thirty. Afghanistan was their first target because bin Laden had a historical connection to the country during the Soviet-Afghan War. He had supported the Afghan mujahideen in their "holy jihad" against the Soviets, training fighters and building his network there. Afghanistan, by then, was a well-established nest for him and his followers.

In first response, the U.S. bombarded the entire country looking for this rat, who eventually fled to Pakistan. If the U.S. were a person, they might have thought: "Oh my God, I’ve destroyed an entire country. It’s now my humanitarian responsibility to rebuild it." This marked the beginning of what the U.S. referred to as "reconstruction"—a term officially documented in their reports.

During this reconstruction phase, the U.S., as an advanced Western civilization with centuries of democratic governance and influence, chose democracy as the political structure for Afghanistan. Why democracy? It was the most logical and familiar system available. It’s the default choice. I would’ve done it too. Don’t lie—you would’ve done the same. If you invaded, say, Djibouti and decided to reconstruct it, you’d probably propose the most famous political structure in the world: democracy. Because you would choose the most default settings possible.

So, when you ask, “Was democracy applicable in Afghanistan?” let me turn the question back to you: What would you have done? Would you have brought back monarchy, like the French in the 19th century? Or installed communism, like the Soviets? Maybe fascism or Nazism? No, you’d choose democracy because it’s the easiest, most default setting available. That’s exactly what the U.S. did—it wasn’t out of kindness or out of force, but out of practicality.

Afghanistan had already tried every political system over the past 50 years, and none worked. Monarchy? → Coup d’états. Communism? → War. Tribal leadership under warlords like Massoud and Gulbuddin? → Chaos and terror. Islamic government under the Taliban? → A safe heaven for rats like Bin Laden, who killed thousands of innocents—not just in the U.S., but everywhere.

So, what would you have chosen? Communism, AGAIN? So the same people could tear the country apart, like they did before? Monarchy, AGAIN? So the same factions could stage a coup d’état like they did before? Tribal Rule, AGAIN? So they could cannibalize each other like they did before? Another Islamic Taliban-style government AGAIN? That would defeat the whole point of “Operation Enduring Freedom,” which wiped them out in the first place.

The U.S. chose the default option: democracy. Not because they wanted to, but because it was the only logical choice available. They weren’t trying to “gift” or “force” democracy upon Afghanistan out of sheer generosity. No, no. They found themselves in a situation where leaving Afghanistan in chaos would have created an even hotter breeding ground for terrorists. Let’s not pretend otherwise—we all saw what happened after the Soviets left. Massoud and Gulbuddin turned Kabul into a war zone. It wasn’t the Soviets who destroyed Kabul; in fact, the Soviets built infrastructure we used for decades, like the bridges over the Behsood River. It was us—our people—who tore the country apart, spreading terror and creating the chaos that led to the Taliban’s rise. That’s another story.

The U.S. got stuck. They couldn’t leave Afghanistan as it was, so still they decided to reconstruct it. But we didn’t accept their reconstruction. For 20 years, we fought and killed each other, more than we fought the Americans. Their soldiers were well-trained, well-equipped, and supported by fucking air demons like Fairchild A-10 Thunderbolt II which still scares me in my dreams, that could wipe out everything in its path to save their men. The ones who died most were our own brothers, killing each other while the U.S. stood on the sidelines.

So no, the U.S. didn’t offer democracy out of the sheer goodness of their hearts, nor did they force it on us to turn us into “infidels,” as some religious zealots like to claim. These religious robots genuinely believe that the U.S. invaded Afghanistan because of our faith—that they saw our “strong iman” as a threat, thinking that if they left us alone, we’d somehow dominate the world and become the next superpower. This delusion blinds them to reality. The truth is, the U.S. got trapped in a situation where they had no better option. And we, instead of focusing on reconstruction and rebuilding our country, chose to fight among ourselves, repeating the same destructive cycles.

If we had chosen what Germany or Japan chose after World War II, we wouldn’t be here today, families scattered across foreign lands where we don’t know the language or the culture. I wouldn’t be here, fucking around in France among people who have no connection to my culture. You, your brother, and his family wouldn’t be living in exile, where our children will eventually forget who we are. Instead, we would be in our own country, building it, educating ourselves, participating in politics. I, you, Nouman—any one of us—might have had a chance to be president of Afghanistan one day. But we didn’t.

We didn’t choose reconstruction because we were told that aligning with the so-called “kafirs” would weaken our iman. We clung to this misguided pride, this self-destructive belief that working with them meant betraying our faith. Look at Germany: the U.S. and its allies obliterated that country during World War II, turning its cities to PURE dust. But once the war ended, Germany didn’t resist reconstruction. They didn’t waste time fighting the Allies who had defeated them. Instead, they stood WITH them to rebuild THEIR country, to rise from the ashes. The U.S. and its allies, knowing they couldn’t leave a power vacuum in a nation that had once been a global threat, set up a stable, democratic government. Together, they rebuilt Germany into one of the world’s strongest economies. If Germany had chosen what we chose, it would be a country like Serbia or Romania, not a world-leading economy today.

And what did we do? The U.S. overthrew the Taliban—a regime of dark terror that executed women in the streets and turned Afghanistan into a global disgrace. Instead of standing with them to rebuild, we resisted. We fought against the very people who liberated us from a nightmare. We chose violence, chaos, and destruction. We chose to fight each other, to kill each other, instead of seizing the opportunity to rebuild our now far, fantastic, only in dreams homeland, that we wouldn’t get a chance to live in it.

And what did we gain? Nothing. All we did was solidify our status as a failed state. I still can’t unsee those historic footages of the Taliban in their first stage, executing women in broad daylight in front of hundreds of men as if it was a sports game. It makes me vomit, even now. We had a chance to escape that darkness, to rebuild and create a future for ourselves and our children. Instead, we let pride, ignorance, and misguided faith hold us back, leaving us scattered and broken, and now here we are, you, me, nouman and thousands of others far from the country we could have called our dear home.

You’re pulling me into a rabbit hole, making me get the flashbacks of my life in Afghanistan. Honestly, I could write about this for the next two hours and still not be done.

So, briefly, to answer your question, "Was U.S.-style democracy an applicable system in Afghanistan?" the answer is yes. It’s not perfect—it’s incomplete, mathematically impossible, and flawed like any other system—but it’s less worse than the alternatives we’ve experienced. We aren’t strangers to political systems. We’ve tried them all: monarchy, communism, tribal leadership, Islamic regimes like the Taliban, and all they brought was more suffering, more destruction, more bloodshed and cannibalism. If democracy is BAD, the rest have been far far far WORSE. So, I’d choose the least bad option for a people who refuse to accept anything else.

Instead of seeing the U.S. presence as just an invasion, I would argue it was a chance—a chance to escape, once and for all, from the terror of the first Taliban regime. But we didn’t take it. Neither did the politicians, who stole everything they could, nor the so-called religious “purists,” who killed their brothers and innocent civilians in the name of faith. We squandered that opportunity, letting greed and dogma drive us further into chaos.

Now, as for your second question—"What makes a political system applicable and legitimate to apply to a group of people?"—I’d say NONE. No political system is justifiable to be imposed on a stable government or people who are already managing their affairs peacefully. But when there is no government, there is no stability—when there is bloodshed, war, terror, and a breeding ground for extremists and terrorists—then what choice do you have? If you’ve invaded a country and you can’t just leave it as a failed state, you must propose the most default, historically successful structure available. In Afghanistan’s case, that was democracy. Not because it was perfect or legitimate to apply, or ideal, but because it was the only logical option in the absence of anything else.
